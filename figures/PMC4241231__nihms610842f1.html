Metabolic Engineering of Salmonella Vaccine Bacteria to Boost Human VG2VD2 T Cell Immunity

There are two pathways for isoprenoid biosynthesis. Most eubacteria, apicomplexan parasitic protozoa, and plant chloroplasts use the MEP pathway. Most eukaryotic species, including humans and other mammals, use the mevalonate pathway. In eubacteria and parasites using the MEP pathway, Vγ2Vδ2 T cells primarily recognize the metabolite HMBPP that is produced by HMBPP synthase (ispG, gcpE) and converted by LytB (hdr, ispH) to IPP and DMAPP. To engineer Salmonella vaccine bacteria, plasmids containing enzymes of the mevalonate pathway from Streptomyces sp. strain CL190 were transformed into the bacteria. The bacteria were then transformed with the pKD46 temperature-sensitive plasmid containing genes for λ Red recombination proteins. The transformants were cultured at 30°C with fosmidomycin (to block the MEP pathway and switch isoprenoid metabolism to the mevalonate pathway). To target the lytB gene, PCR amplification with primers flanking the lytB gene were used to generate a 1.1 kb linear fragment encoding the chloramphenicol resistance gene flanked by 50 base pairs of 5′ and 3′ sequence adjacent to the lytB gene. L-arabinose was then added to induce the recombination enzymes and the DNA fragment targeting the lytB gene introduced into the bacteria by electroporation. Chloramphenicol was used to select for lytB deletion mutants that were then grown at 37°C to eliminate the pKD46 plasmid. Deleting lytB causes HMBPP to accumulate while the addition of the mevalonate pathway allows the bacteria to survive.
