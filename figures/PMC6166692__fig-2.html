Redox-Sensitive Innate Immune Pathways During Macrophage Activation in Type 1 Diabetes

Redox-regulated inflammatory signaling during macrophage activation. Recognition of certain PAMPs by TLRs on either the cell surface or within the endosome will activate either MyD88-mediated (TLR2, 4, 7, 8, 9) or TRIF-mediated (TLR3) NF-κB pathway activation (A). This includes phosphorylation and subsequent proteasomal degradation of IκBα, in turn allowing for the phosphorylation and heterodimerization of NF-κB-p65 with phosphorylated NF-κB-p50 subunit. The p50/p65 heterodimer then translocates to the nucleus to initiate transcription of inflammatory genes. Also on pathogen recognition, the NOX2 complex becomes activated at the membrane, and initiates a superoxide-derived oxidative burst, producing free radicals that enhance phosphorylation of IκBα and NF-κB-p65 to enhance signaling. SUMO4 negatively regulates signaling by stabilizing IκBα activity and inhibiting NF-κB-p50 DNA binding. Inflammatory IFNγ in the local milieu initiates the STAT1 pathway to activate the inflammatory M1 macrophage response (B). After IFNγ/IFNGR interaction, phosphorylation and activation of Jak1/Jak2 then phosphorylate and activate STAT1, allowing for homodimerization and nuclear translocation to initiate an M1 transcriptional profile. Production of free radicals on activation can enhance STAT1 signaling by oxidizing and inhibiting the activity of several PTPs that can dephosphorylate and inactivate STAT1. IκBα, NF-kB inhibitor alpha; IFN, interferon; IFNGR, IFNγ receptor; JAK, janus kinase; PAMP, pathogen-associated molecular pattern; PTP, protein tyrosine phosphatase; STAT, signal transducer and activator of transcription; SUMO, small ubiquitin-like modifier; TLR, Toll-like receptor; TRIF, TIR-domain-containing adapter-inducing interferon-beta.
