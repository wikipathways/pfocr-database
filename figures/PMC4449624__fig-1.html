Metabolic Alterations Induce Oxidative Stress in Diabetic and Failing Hearts: Different Pathways, Same Outcome

FFA and carbohydrate metabolisms in the cardiomyocyte. FFA enter cardiomyocyte via FAT/CD36. In the cytosol, FFA are converted into TG for storage or into FA-CoA esters by FACS and successively conjugated with carnitine by CPT-1 to form fatty acylcarnitine. CPT-1 is the key-limiting step determining FFA entry in mitochondria and its activity is regulated by the inhibitor malonyl-CoA. The concentration of malonyl-CoA depends on the balance between its synthesis catalyzed by ACC and its degradation catalyzed by MCD. Fatty acylcarnitine enters mitochondria via CT and is converted into FA-CoA by CPT-2. In mitochondria, FA-CoA undergo complete degradation in the β-oxidation cycle, leading to the production of acetyl-CoA, which is then catabolized in the tricarboxylic acid cycle to generate FADH2 and NADH, that is, the reducing equivalents feeding the electron (e−) transport chain. Glucose enters cardiomyocytes via the GLUT1 and GLUT4 (GLUT1/4). Once in the cardiomyocyte, glucose is phosphorylated by HK. G-6P derived from exogenous glucose and the intracellular glycogen pool undergo progressive breakdown through the glycolytic pathway or, to a lesser extent, the oxPPP and the polyol pathway. Most of the pyruvate generated from glycolysis or lactate dehydrogenation is decarboxylated in mitochondria to form acetyl-CoA. This reaction is catalyzed by PDH.
