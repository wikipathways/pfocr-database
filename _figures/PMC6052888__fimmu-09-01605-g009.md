caption: 'Antioxidants neutralize mitochondrial ROS (mtROS). (1) Antioxidants in mitochondria:
  superoxide  is released from complex I (into the matrix) and III (into both the
  matrix and intermembrane space). This is converted to H2O2 in the presence of superoxide
  dismutase (SOD). Mn-SOD (manganese dismutase) is localized in mitochondria matrix
  and Cu/Zn-SOD (in the intermembrane space and cytosol). SIRT3 is involved in deacetylation
  of MnSOD. Catalase converts H2O2 to H2O. Catalase is also localized in mitochondria
  (MCAT). Glutathione converts OH− to H2O to reduce the cellular damage. Glutathione
  (GSH) is oxidized and reduced to glutathione disulfide (GSSG) in a reversible reaction.
  NADPH from the pentose phosphate pathway reduces GSSG to form GSH in presence of
  glutathione peroxidase (Gpx). (2) Antioxidants play an important role in neutralizing
  mtROS. Uncoupling protein 2 (UCP2) (mitochondrial uncoupler protein), which helps
  to shuttle H+ ions from the intermembrane space to the mitochondria matrix, decreases
  the membrane potential to inhibit excessive reactive oxygen species (ROS) production.
  Excessive ROS production can impact ATP synthesis, cytokine production, mitochondrial
  DNA (mtDNA) mutation, and post-translational modification of cellular proteins by
  S-nitosylation or glutathionylation.'
